All + All -

A - Buddhist Psychology (tib.: blo rigs)

  • + - A1. Why Study Mind?

    • + - Both the world of sentients and non-sentients operate in all the multiple shades purely driven by the mind. The Dhammapada says:

      • Mind is the chief and precedes them all;
        If with impure mind, one acts or speaks,
        Miseries follows
        Like a cart following the ox.

        Mind is the chief and precedes them all;
        If with pure mind, one acts or speaks,
        Happiness follows
        Like a shadow that never leaves.

      + - In Abhidharmakosha, Acharya Vasubhandu said:

      • The diversity of the world arose from karmas,
        Which in turn is, the intention and its actions.
        Intention is the mental karma;
        What gives rise to by (the intention) are physical and verbal.

      + - Acharya Chandrakirti said in Madhyamakavatara

      • It is the mind itself that constructed the sentient world
        As well as the utterly diverse container world;
        So [the Buddha] taught that all beings are produced from karma;
        Without mind, there can be no karma.

      + - His Holiness the Dalai Lama says in a dialogue with Western scientists in 1991:

      • “…There are two reasons why it is important to understand the nature of mind. One is because there is an innate connection between mind and karma (our actions.) The other is that our state of mind plays a crucial role in our experience of happiness and suffering.B1” It is therefore essential to understand the mind to systematically purifying and enhancing the mind for the process of achieving enlightenment.

  • + - A2. Definition and the Nature of Mind

    • + - The definition of mind or consciousness is that which is clear and Knowing.

      • + - His Holiness the Dalai Lama describes the intrinsic connection between the clear and knowing aspects of the mind this way:

        • The knowing nature, or agency … is called mind and this is nonmaterial … Cognitive events possess the nature of knowing because of the fundamental nature of clarity that underlies all cognitive events. This is … the mind’s fundamental nature, the clear-light nature of mind.

      Mind (skt.:chitta; tib.:sem) can be classified in a number of different ways; a major way of division is known as seven fold division of mind.

  • + - A3. Seven Fold Division of Mind (tib.:blo rigs bdun)
    • + - A3.1) Direct Valid Perceiver (skt.:pratyaksh; tib.:mngon sum)
      • A non-mistaken knower that is free from conceptuality.
      • Direct valid perceivers are of four types:
      • + - a) Sense Direct Perceiver (skt.:indriya-pratyaksh; tib.:dbang po’i mngon sum)
        • + - A non-mistaken non-conceptual knower that is produced upon the aggregation of three conditions:

          • 1) Observed object condition (skt.:alambana-pratyaya; tib.:dmigs rkyen)

            2) Uncommon empowering condition (skt.:asadhara adhipatipratyaya; tib.:thun mong ma yin pa’i bdag rkyen)

            3) Immediately preceding condition (skt.:samanantara-pratyaya; tib.:de ma thag rkyen)

        • + - Five Types of Sense Direct Perceivers
          • i. Visual Sense Consciousness (skt.:cakṣur-vijñana; tib.:mig gi shes pa)
          • ii. Auditory Sense Consciousness (skt.:śrotra-vijñana; tib.:rna ba'i shes pa)
          • iii. Olfactory Sense Consciousness (skt.:ghrāṇa-vijñana; tib.:sna'i shes pa)
          • iv. Gustatory Sense Consciousness (skt.:jihva-vijñana; tib.:lce'i rnam shes pa)
          • v. Tactile Sense Consciousness (skt.:kāya-vijñana; tib:lus kyi rnam shes pa)
      • + - b) Mental Direct Perceiver (skt.:manasa-pratyaksh; tib.:yid kyi mngon sum)

        • A non-mistaken non-conceptual knower that arises from its own mental sense power as its uncommon empowering condition.

          Types of mental direct perceivers:

          + - i. Those that are indicated in the Sutra

          • + - Mental direct perceivers depending on the minds

            • i.e. those induced by sense direct perceivers apprehending any of the five sense objects- forms, sounds, odors, tastes, or tactile objects.

          + - ii. Those that are not indicated in the Sutra

          • + - Mental direct perceivers induced by states arisen from meditation such as the five clairvoyances.

            • (1) The clairvoyance of magical emanation (skt.:rddhi-abhijna; tib.:rdsu ‘phrul gyi mngon shes)

              (2) The clairvoyance of divine eye (skt.:divya-chaksur-abhijna; tib.:lha’i mig gi mngon shes)

              (3) The clairvoyance of divine ear (skt.:divya-shrotra-abhijna; tib.:lha’i rna ba’i mngon shes)

              (4) The clairvoyance of former lives (skt.:purva-nivasanusmriti-abhijnana; tib.:sngon gyi gnas rjes su dran pa’i mngon shes)

              (5) The clairvoyance of reading others’ mind (skt.:para-chitta-jnana-abhijna; tib.:gzan sems shes pa’i mngon shes)

      • + - c) Self-cognizant Direct Perceiver (skt.:svasamvedana-pratyaksh; tib.:rang rig mngon sum)
        • A non-mistaken non-conceptual knower that has the aspect of an apprehender.
        • + - Self-cognizant direct perceivers can be divided into three types:

          • i. Those that are prime/ valid cognizers.
            ii. Those that are subsequent cognizers.
            iii.Those which are non-discerning direct perceivers.

      • + - d) Yogic Direct Perceiver (skt.:yogi-pratyaksh; tib.:rnal ‘byor mngon sum)

        • A non-mistaken non-conceptual exalted knower in the continuum of a superior that is produced from a meditative stabilization which is a union of calm abiding and special insight and which has become its own uncommon empowering condition.

          Yogic direct perceivers can be divided in various ways:

          + - i. From the viewpoint of the person who possesses it

          • Yogic direct perceivers of hearers (skt.:shravaka; tib.:nyan thos), solitary realizers (skt.:pratyekabuddha; tib.:rang rgyal) and Mahayanists.

          + - ii. From the viewpoint of their entities

          • Yogic direct perceivers of path of seeing, path of meditation and path of no more learning.

          + - iii. From the viewpoint of their aspects

          • One can posit sixteen kinds of yogic direct perceivers, having the aspects of sixteen attributes of the four noble truths such as impermanence, suffering, emptiness, selflessness and so forth.

          + - iv. From the viewpoint of having aspect of coarse and subtle selflessness

          • With respect to selflessness as the object of yogic direct perception, on top of the yogic direct perception viewing the subtle selflessness of autonomous substantial reality, some scholars also include yogic direct perception viewing the gross selflessness in the context of selflessness of permanent, unitary and independent self.

            Chittamatra and Madhyamaka schools include the yogic direct perceiver which views the selflessness of phenomena also. As indicated by Acharya Kamalashila, these two schools identify all teachings of the Buddha to either point towards the ultimate reality, or lead towards the ultimate reality or establishes the ultimate reality. The teachings on impermanence, misery and so forth, belong to the first category, whereas the teachings on the selflessness of person in the context of emptiness of autonomous substantial sell and the emptiness of permanent, unitary and independent self belong to the second category and the teachings on the selflessness of phenomena fall under the third category to establishes the ultimate truth.

    • + - A3.2) Inferential Cognizer (skt.:anuman; tib.:rjes dpag)

      • A determinative knower, which by dependence on a correct sign as its basis, is incontrovertible with regard to a hidden phenomenon as its object of comprehension.

        + - There are three types of inferential cognizers by way of their entities:

        • + - a) Inference through the power of fact (skt.:vastu-bala-anuman; tib.:dngos stobs rjes dpag)

          • A determinative knower, which by dependence on a correct sign by the power of a fact as its basis, is incontrovertible with respect to a slightly hidden phenomenon as its object of comprehension.

        • + - b) Inference through confidence (skt.:apta-anuman; tib.:yid ches rjes dpag)

          • A determinative knower, which by dependence on a correct sign of confidence as its basis, is incontrovertible with regard to a very hidden phenomenon as its object of comprehension.

        • + - c) Inference through renown (Convention) (skt.:prasiddha-anuman; tib.:grags pa’i rjes dpag)

          • A determinative knower, which by dependence on a correct sign of renown as its basis, is incontrovertible with respect to a terminological suitability as its object of comprehension.

    • + - A3.3) Subsequent Cognizer (skt.:parichchhinna-jnana; tib.:bcad shes)

      • + - A knower which is not a prime cognizer and which realizes what has already been realized by the former prime cognizer inducing it.
        It is also defined as ‘a knower which realizes that which has already been realized’.

        • The first moment of a consciousness engages with an object through its own power and is thus known as prime cognizer.
          Whereas the subsequent moments realize their object merely by the force of the preceding prime cognizer that induced them.
          Therefore they are referred to as subsequent cognizers.

        + - Subsequent cognizers are of two types:

        • + - a) Direct Subsequent Cognizer (skt.:pratyaksh-parichchhinna-jnana; tib.:mgon sum bcad shes)
          • + - i. Sense Direct Subsequent Cognizer
            • An illustration is the second moment of a sense direct perceiver apprehending a form and so forth.
          • + - ii. Mental Direct Subsequent Cognizer

            • An illustration is the second moment of a mental direct perceiver in the continuum of a Superior which is apprehending a form and so forth as they are smallest moment and for ordinary beings are awareness to which an object appears but is not ascertained.

          • + - iii.Self-cognizant Direct Subsequent Cognizer

            • An illustration is the second moment of a self-cognizer which is experiencing a sense direct perceiver apprehending a form and so forth.

          • + - iv.Yogic Direct Subsequent Cognizer

            • An illustration is the second moment of the yogic direct perceiver directly realizing the selflessness of person.

        • + - b) Conceptual Subsequent Cognizer (skt.:kalpana-parichchhinna-jnana; tib.:rtog pa bcad shes)
          • + - i. Those induced by direct perception

            • A conceptual subsequent cognizer induced by direct perception is, for example, a consciousness ascertaining blue which is produced subsequent to a direct perceiver apprehending blue.

          • + - ii. Those induced by inference

            • A conceptual subsequent cognizer induced by inference is, for example, the second moment of an inferential consciousness that realizes sound to be impermanent.

    • + - A3.4) Correctly Assuming Consciousness (skt.:manah-pariksha; tib.:yid dpyod)

      • An awareness that does not cognize an object with respect to which super-impositions have been eliminated although it adheres onepointedly to the phenomenon which is its principal object of engagement.

        + - Correctly Assuming Consciousness with respect to any thesis for example, ‘Sound is impermanent’ can be generated in three ways:

        • + - a) Without a reason

          • A correctly assuming consciousness without a reason is an awareness which thinks, ‘Sound is impermanent,’ without any reason at all. This is not something that is ascertained or realized, but is merely thought in an unreasoned manner.

        • + - b) Without ascertaining the reason

          • A correctly assuming consciousness without ascertaining the reason is an awareness which thinks, ‘Sound is impermanent,’ based on a sign of being a product, but it has not ascertained that it is a product and that whatever is a product is necessarily impermanent. (It has not ascertained the presence of the reason in the subject or the forward and counter pervasions and thus the three modes are not established)

        • + - c) Depending on a facsimile of reason

          • A correctly assuming consciousness depending on a facsimile of a reason is an awareness which thinks, ‘Sound is impermanent,’ from the sign of being an object of comprehension. Thus, in this type of correctly assuming consciousness, there is no pervasion between the reason and the predicate of the thesis – whatever is an object of comprehension is not necessarily impermanent because there are objects of comprehension which are impermanent and objects of comprehension that are permanent.

    • + - A3.5) Non-Discerning Direct Perceiver (skt.:aniyata-pratibha; tib.:snang la ma nges pa)

      • A knower to which the specifically characterized phenomenon (impermanent phenomenon) which is its object of engagement clearly appears but is unable to induce ascertainment with respect to it.

        + - Nondiscerning direct perceivers are of three types:

        • + - a) Non-discerning Sense Direct Perceiver

          • An illustration of the sense direct perceiver to which an object appears but is not ascertained, is the sense direct perceiver apprehending blue which induces the doubt, ‘Did I see blue or not?’

        • + - b) Non-discerning Mental Direct Perceiver

          • An illustration of the mental direct perceiver to which an object appears but is not ascertained, is the mental direct perceiver apprehending a form or so forth in the continuum of an ordinary being.

        • + - c) Non-discerning Self-cognizant Direct Perceiver

          • An illustration of self-cognizant direct perceiver to which an object appears but is not ascertained, is the self-cognizer experiencing the mental direct perceiver apprehending a form or so forth in the continuum of an ordinary being.

    • + - A3.6) Doubting Consciousness (skt.:samshaya; tib.:the tshom)
      • A knower which by its own power has qualms in two directions.
      • + - Doubting consciousnesses are of three types:
        • + - a) Doubt tending towards the fact

          • Doubting consciousness tending towards fact is, for example, a two-pointed mind thinking, ‘Sound is probably impermanent’.

        • + - b) Doubt tending towards distortion

          • Doubting consciousness tending towards distortion is, for example, a two-pointed mind thinking, ‘Sound is probably permanent’

        • + - c) Doubt tending towards both equally

          • Doubting consciousness tending towards both equally is, for example, a hesitating consciousness which wonders whether sound is permanent or impermanent.

    • + - A3.7) Deceptive [Wrong] Consciousness (skt.:viparyaya-jnana; tib.:log shes)
      • A knower which is mistaken with regard to its object of engagement.
      • + - There are two types of deceptive consciousness:
        • + - a) Conceptual Deceptive Consciousness

          • For example, a thought consciousness apprehending horns of a rabbit and a consciousness apprehending selfhood of person.

        • + - b) Non-conceptual Deceptive Consciousness
          • + - i. Non-conceptual Sense Deceptive Consciousness

            • For example, a sense consciousness seeing two moons or a sense consciousness to which Snow Mountains appear blue.

          • + - ii. Non-conceptual Mental Deceptive Consciousness
            • For example, a dream consciousness to which blue clearly appears.
  • + - A4. Three Fold Division of Mind
    • + - A4.1 Conceptual Consciousness (tib.:rtog pa)

      • A mind that apprehends its object through mixing it with sound/meaning generality.

        Conceptual consciousness is always a mistaken mind for it is mistaken with respect to appearance of the object. But, it may or may not be deceptive, for example, conceptual experience of emptiness is a mistaken mind but not a deceptive mind whereas self grasping ignorance is deceptive for it is mistaken with respect to object of engagement.

    • + - A4.2 Non-conceptual Non-mistaken Consciousness
      • Awareness that takes a specifically characterized phenomenon as their apprehended object
    • + - A4.3 Non-conceptual Mistaken Consciousness

      • Awareness that takes a clearly appearing  (non-conceptual) non-existent as its apprehended object – This is synonymous with non-conceptual deceptive [wrong] consciousness.

  • + - A5. Two Fold Divisions of Mind

    • + - A5.1 Prime Cognizer and Non-prime Consciousness (tib.:tsed ma dang tsed min)

      • + - a. Prime Cognizer

        • A prime cognizer is defined as a knower which is new and incontrovertible.

          Some examples from within the seven fold division of mind are– first moment of direct valid perceivers and inferential cognizers.

        + - b. non-prime consciousness

        • The definition of non-prime consciousness is a knower which is not new and incontrovertible.

          Subsequent cognizers, correctly assuming consciousnesses, doubting consciousnesses, non-discerning direct perceivers and deceptive [wrong] consciousnesses are the examples of non-prime consciousnesses.

        This is an exhaustive division of awareness and knower as any specific consciousness is one or the other. Limiting the types of prime cognitions to two in this way is specifically done to set the Buddhist view off from that of various non-Buddhist systems, which accept many other sources of prime, or valid cognition such as sound, example and so forth.

      + - A5.2 Conceptual Consciousness and Non-conceptual Consciousness

      • This is an exhaustive division of awareness and knower. The emphasis here being on the manner in which a consciousness apprehends its object – either directly (non-conceptual consciousness) or by means of a generic image (conceptual consciousness)

        Within seven fold division of mind, all inferential cognizers, correctly assuming consciousnesses, and doubting consciousnesses, as well as some subsequent cognizers and some deceptive [wrong] consciousnesses, are posited as the illustrations of conceptual consciousnesses.
        The remaining awarenesses and knowers – all direct valid perceivers and nondiscerning direct perceivers as well as some part of subsequent cognizers and also some part of deceptive [wrong] consciousnesses are the examples of non-conceptual consciousnesses.

      + - A5.3 Mistaken Consciousness (tib.:‘khrul shes) and Non-Mistaken Consciousness

      • This division is made in terms of whether – a consciousness is mistaken with regard to its appearing or apprehended object (mistaken consciousness) or is non- mistaken with regard to its appearing or apprehended object (non-mistaken consciousness) as opposed to their object of engagement. -

        Among seven types of knower and awareness, all non-mistaken nonconceptual consciousnesses i.e. direct valid perceivers are non-mistaken consciousnesses.
        And, all conceptual consciousnesses are included within mistaken consciousness inasmuch the image of the object they are comprehending appears to them to be the actual object.
        Non-conceptual deceptive [wrong] consciousnesses are also mistaken as their appearing object and object of engagement appear as same thing which is wrong.

      + - A5.4 Mental Consciousness (tib.:yid shes) and Sense Consciousness (tib.:dbang shes)

      • This twofold division is made in terms of whether a consciousness is produced in dependence on a mental sense power (mental consciousness) or a physical sense power such as eye, ear and so forth as its uncommon empowering condition.

        Sense consciousnesses are necessarily non-conceptual;
        mental consciousness can either be conceptual or non-conceptual: Mental, selfcognizer and yogic are all non-conceptual mental consciousnesses whereas inference, correct assumption, doubt and so forth are conceptual mental consciousnesses.

      + - A5.5 Eliminative Engager Awareness (tib.:sel ‘jug) and Collective Engager Awareness (tib.:sgrub ‘jug)

      • This division, again exhaustive, resembles the division into conceptual and non-conceptual consciousnesses but here the emphasis is on the manner in which that consciousness apprehends its object.

        + - a) Eliminative Engager

        • It is a mind that engages its object by the power of label. The word ‘label’ here refers to a meaning generality.

          Eliminative engager engages its object in a negative or eliminative manner, thus, it can never come to perceive all the uncommon features of its object such as its impermanence, momentariness and so forth. They are synonymous with conceptual consciousnesses.

        + - b) Collective Engager

        • It is a mind that engages with its object by the power of the object, which is the object’s casting its aspect to the mind apprehending it.

          It engages its object in a positive or collective manner, thus all the uncommon features of the object such as its impermanence, momentariness and so forth, appear to that consciousness. However, the mere appearance of all these to the consciousness does not mean that they are necessarily ascertained; most are not noticed due to the interference of thought and predispositions.

      + - A5.6 Minds and Mental Factors

      • This twofold division is a way of describing the various functions of consciousness.

        + - a) Mind

        • Mind (skt.:chitta; tib.:sems) here is synonymous with main mind or primary mind (tib.:gtso sems).The definition of mind [in the context of mind and mental factors] is an awareness which has similarity with the mental factors that arise as its accompaniers. This can also be understood as an awareness which apprehends the mere entity of the object of engagement. Some examples of the main minds are the five sense consciousnesses and mental consciousness.

        + - b) Mental Factors

        • A mental factor (skt.:chaitta; tib.:sems byung) is defined as the aspect of awareness that engages with its object with different functions of the awareness. The definition of a mental factor [in the context of mind and mental factors] is an awareness which has similarity with the primary mind that has it as an accompanier.

        + - Minds and mental factors are accompanied by each other and have five commonalities between them, with respect to any particular object.

        • 1) They have a common object of observation and thus are similar with regard to object of engagement.

          2) They are generated in the same aspect, in that if eye consciousness is generated in the aspect of blue, the accompanying mental factors are also generated in the aspect of blue.

          3) They exist in the same time, in that when one is produced the other is produced.

          4) They have common uncommon empowering condition and thus have a common basis.

          5) They are the same substantial entity, in that the production, abiding and cessation of the two occur simultaneously, for example, only a single substantial entity of feeling arises as the accompanier of a single substantial entity of an eye perceiver.

        There are innumerable mental factors each with a specific function that relates to its object. In the Sanskrit Nalanda tradition one finds the mention of fifty one mental factors whereas in the Pali tradition there is a mention of fifty two mental factors, enumeration of which is enclosed as appendage to the text.

  • + - A6. Fifty One Mental Factors

    • Mental factors as enumerated in the Abhidharma of Sanskrit Nalanda tradition are classified in six groups:

      + - A6.1) Five Omnipresent Mental Factors (skt.:sarvatraga; tib.:kun ‘gro)

      • The following five mental factors are known as omnipresent mental factors as they accompany every primary mind.

        + - 1) Feeling (skt.:vedanā; tib.:tshor ba)

        • This mental factor assumes the experience of either pleasure, pain or neutrality.

        + - 2) Discrimination (skt.:samjnā; tib.:‘du shes)

        • Mental factor with the functionality to apprehend the uncommon signs or characteristics of an object, to distinguish the given object from other phenomena.

        + - 3) Intention (skt.:chetnā; tib.:sems pa)

        • It directs the activity of each of the other factors within the main mind in respect to the object.

        + - 4) Contact (skt.:sparsha; tib.:reg pa)

        • A mental factor with the functionality to give rise to the changes of the senses for the three kinds of feelings through coming into contact of the object, sense power and consciousness.

        + - 5) Attention (skt.:manasi-kāra; tib.:yid la byed pa)

        • This mental factor directs the mind to the particular object of observation.

      + - A6.2) Five Object-ascertaining / Determinative Mental Factors (skt.:vishayapratiniyama; tib.:yul nges)

      • These mental factors help ascertain the object of the main mind. If one of these is present, all five are present; however these do not accompany all minds. How close the ascertained object is to the reality depends on how enlightened or deluded the ascertaining mind is.

        + - 1) Aspiration (skt.:chhanda; tib.:‘dun pa)

        • Aspiration, like intention, directs the mind towards an object. It is the mind that wishes to engage in a particular activity and takes a strong interest in the process.

          Although intention and aspiration are similar, intention acts as one of the fundamental aspects of any mind, whereas aspiration is the result of many processes occurring and is not always present.

        + - 2) Admiration (skt.:adhimoksha; tib.:mos pa)

        • Seeing that the ascertained object has qualities that are worthwhile (can be positive or negative), admiration stabilizes the relationship with the object by directing the mind toward it more forcefully.

        + - 3) Recollection / Mindfulness (skt.:smrti; tib.:dran pa)

        • It is the ability of the mind to return to the object.

          The continuous application of mindfulness acts as the basis for concentration and is also the basis for memory.

        + - 4) Concentration (skt.:samādhi; tib.:ting nge ‘dzin)

        • Concentration in this context is the ability of the mind to remain on the object.

        + - 5) Wisdom / Intelligence (skt.:prajnā; tib.:shes rab)

        • It is the ability of the mind to examine an object and determine its value, by seeing that object has certain characteristics that make it attractive, repulsive or neither.

      + - A6.3) Eleven Virtuous Mental Factors (skt.:kushala; tib.:dge ba)

      • + - 1) Faith (skt.:shraddhā; tib.:dad pa)
        • It is a mental factor of finding joy which is freed of root and secondary afflictions.
        • + - This mental factor is the main entrance to all virtues. It is of three types:
          • a. Faith of admiration
          • b. Faith of aspiration
          • c. Faith of conviction
      • + - 2) Sense of shame out of self-respect (skt.:hri; tib.:ngo tsha shes pa)

        • It is a mental factor that helps the individual to refrain from negativities due to respect for oneself.

      • + - 3) Sense of shame out of respect for others (skt.:apatrāpya; tib.:khrel yod pa)

        • This mental factor helps one to refrain from negativities due to respect for others.

          The two mental factors – sense of shame out of self-respect and sense of shame out of respect for others – serve as basis for unspoiled moral discipline.

      • + - 4) Non-attachment (skt.:alobha; tib.:chagpa med pa)
        • It is a mental factor that is free of attachment to cyclic existence or mundane objects.
      • + - 5) Non-aversion (skt.:advesha; tib.:zhe sdang med pa)

        • It is a mental factor which is free of animosity to three objects:
          1. perpetrators
          2. suffering
          3. cause of suffering such as poison, thorn and so forth

      • + - 6) Non-ignorance (skt.:amoha; tib.:gti mug med pa)
        • It is a mental factor that is free from confusions.
      • + - 7) Enthusiasm / Perseverance / Joyous effort (skt.:virya; tib.:brtson ‘grus)
        • It is a mental factor that finds joy in virtue.
      • + - 8) Pliancy / Suppleness (skt.:prasrabdhi; tib.:shin tu sbyangs pa)

        • This mental factor is characterized by the quality of serviceability of the mind on a virtuous object. It enables the mind to engage in positive acts as wished, such as meditation.

      • + - 9) Conscientiousness (skt.:apramāda; tib.:bag yod pa)
        • This mental factor helps the individual to refrain from negativities and engage in virtues.
      • + - 10) Equanimity (skt.:upekshā; tib.:btang snyoms)
        • Equanimity here refers to a stable mind, free of mental dullness or excitement.
      • + - 11) Harmlessness (skt.:ahimsā; tib.:rnam par mi ‘tshe ba)

        • This mental factor enables the mind to refrain from physical, verbal and mental harmful actions. It is of the nature of compassion.

      + - A6.4) Six Root Afflictions (skt.:mulaklesha; tib.:rtsa nyon)

      • Affliction or delusion is defined as any mental factor the presence of which disturbs the mind.
      • + - 1) Attachment / Desire (skt.:rāga; tib.:‘dod chags)

        • Attachment exaggerates the positive qualities of the object and moves the mind towards that object.

          Attachment to aggregates in cyclic existence causes rebirth & incessant suffering in samsara.

          Note: Bear in mind that the words attachment and desire have ambiguity in their usage. Some understand these two words as negative whereas others interpret them to have both positive and negative connotation. For example, desire for Buddhahood or Enlightenment is virtuous and positive, whereas desiring to harm others is non-virtuous and negative, thus qualifying afflictive desire as a part of the six root afflictions.

      • + - 2) Anger / Aversion (skt.:pratigha; tib.:khong khro)

        • Anger exaggerates the negative qualities of the object and moves the mind away from that object. It can lead to harming others.

      • + - 3) Pride (skt.:māna tib.:nga rgyal)

        • It is an inflated sense of superiority which is supported by the worldly view of perishable collection towards the self and mine.

          This includes disrespect of others. It is of seven kinds as indicated in Abhidharmakoşha: pride, pride of superiority, extreme pride, pride of ego, empty pride, pride of inferiority, and pride of wrong view.

      • + - 4) Ignorance (skt.:avidhyā; tib.:ma rig pa)

        • This mental factor is characterized by mere absence of knowledge according to Arya Asanga and distortion of the reality according to Acharya Vasubandhu.

          This includes not knowing karma, meaning and practice pertaining to Three Jewels. This can lead to closed-mindedness. -

      • + - 5) Afflictive view (skt.:drshti; tib.:lta ba nyon mongs can)
        • + - Afflictive view is of five kinds:

          • a) View of perishable collection (skt.:satkayadrsti; tib.:‘jig-lta)

            b) View of the extremes (skt.:antagrahadrsti; tib.:mthar-lta)

            c) Distorted view (skt.:drstiparamarsa; tib.:log-lta)

            d) View of Supremacy of view (skt.:mithyadrsti; tib.:lta-ba- mchog-‘dzin)

            e) View of Supremacy of morality and conduct(skt.:silavrataparamarsadrsti; tib.:tsul-khrims-brtul-zhugs- mchog-‘dzin)

      • + - 6) Afflictive doubt (skt.:vichikitsā; tib.:the tshom)

        • This mental factor is characterized by being in two minds about reality, the Triple Gem and so forth.

          It leads one to engage in negativities. Doubt with respect to neutral things is not classified as afflictive doubt.

      + - A6.5) Twenty Secondary Afflictions (skt.:upaklesha; tib.:nye nyon)

      • + - 1) Belligerence (skt.:krodha; tib.:khro ba)

        • It is a malicious state of mind that wishes to cause immediate harm to others due to increased anger.

      • + - 2) Resentment (skt.:upanāha; tib.:‘khon ‘dzin)

        • It causes one not to forget harm done by a person and seeks to return harm done. It is closer to anger in nature.

      • + - 3) Concealment (skt.:mraksha; tib.:‘chab pa)
        • Hiding one’s negative qualities when others with good intention refer to them. This causes regret.
      • + - 4) Spite (skt.:pradāsa; tib.:‘tshig pa)

        • It is intention to utter harsh speech in reply to unpleasant words, when wrath and hatred become unbearable upon others with good intention point to his/her faults.

      • + - 5) Envy / Jealousy (skt.:irshyā; tib.:phrag dog)

        • It is an internal irritation caused by feeling of unease over others’ success and so forth. This is caused by attachment to possessions, respect and so forth. This is responsible for the decline of one’s own good qualities.

      • + - 6) Miserliness (skt.:mātsarya; tib.:ser sna)

        • This mental state is characterized by intense clinging to possessions and their increase due to attachment.

      • + - 7) Dishonesty / Deceit (skt.:māyā; tib.:sgyu)

        • It is a mental state which has the attribute of hiding one’s faults to deceive others due to excessive attachment to possessions, service and so forth. This is closer to attachment and ignorance in nature. This leads to wrong livelihood.

      • + - 8) Pretension / Dissimulation (skt.:sāthya; tib.:gyo)

        • This mental factor superimposes non-existent qualities upon oneself to deceive others due to excessive attachment to possessions, service and so forth. This is closer to attachment and ignorance in nature.

      • + - 9) Vanity / Haughtiness (skt.:mada; tib.:rgyags pa)

        • This mental factor gives a false sense of confidence by seeing one’s good qualities such as fortune, power and so forth. This inhibits the growth of good qualities within oneself. This is closer to attachment in nature.

      • + - 10) Harmfulness (skt.:vihimsā; tib.:rnam par ‘tshe ba)

        • A mental factor which seeks to cause harm on others being devoid of compassion. This is closer to anger in nature.

      • + - 11) Shamelessness (skt.:āhrikya; tib.:ngo tsha med pa)

        • It is a lack of consideration for oneself and consciously not avoiding evil. It supports all root and secondary afflictions.

      • + - 12) Non-embarrassment (skt.:anapatrāpya; tib.:khrel med pa)
        • It is a mental state which does not avoid evil by being inconsiderate of others.
      • + - 13) Dullness / Lethargy (skt.:styāna; tib.:rmugs pa)

        • This is the fogginess of mind which makes the mind heavy and lax. The coarse dullness is when the object is unclear whereas the subtle dullness has some degree of clarity but the vividness of the object is lacking.

      • + - 14) Excitement (skt.:auddhatya; tib.:rgod pa)

        • It distracts one towards desire objects and does not allow the mind to rest on something wholesome. It obstructs one from achieving single pointed concentration.

      • + - 15) Faithlessness (skt.:āshraddhya; tib.:ma dad pa)

        • This mental factor is characterized by lacking belief in those objects which are the basis for virtues such as law of karma. This is closer to ignorance in nature. This is the basis for losing the root of virtues.

      • + - 16) Laziness (skt.:kausidya; tib.:le lo)

        • It is a mental factor which does not find joy in virtues or feels low in engaging in virtues due to adherence to temporary happiness and pleasure.

          This is closer to ignorance in nature and undermines the power of enthusiasm. Laziness is the basis for degenerating the virtues already accumulated and destroys the basis for creating new virtues.

          + - Laziness is of three kinds:

          • a) Laziness of being attached to trivial purposes.
          • b) Laziness of procrastination.
          • c) Laziness of undermining one’s own potential.
      • + - 17) Unconscientiousness (skt.:pramāda; tib.:bag med pa)
        • This is a mental factor which acts in negativities unrestrained.
        • This serves as the basis to diminish virtues and proliferate negativities.
      • + - 18) Forgetfulness (skt.:mu shitasmrtitā; tib.:brjed nges pa)

        • This mental factor causes one to not remember objects of virtues and induces distraction to afflictive objects.

      • + - 19)Non-introspection (skt.:asamprajanya; tib.:shes bzhin ma yin pa)

        • This mental factor is a distorted intelligence that rashly engages in the actions through the three doors either due to lack of analysis or faulty analysis.

          This inhibits wisdom.

      • + - 20)Distraction / Mental wandering / Discursiveness (skt.:vikshepa; tib.:rnam par gyeng ba)

        • A mental factor which functions to be scattered to varied objects due to lack of potential to focus on virtuous objects.

          This diminishes the power of concentration.

      + - A6.6) Four Variable Mental Factors (skt.:anyathābhāva; tib.:gzhan ‘ gyur)

      • Mental factors in this category are called variable mental factors because they can be changed into virtuous, non-virtuous, or neutral types due to either motivation or other accompanying mental factors.

        + - 1) Sleep (skt.:middha; tib.:gnyid)

        • It is the mind that usually operates when our gross sensory consciousnesses cease their functions.

          According to the Abhidharma texts, sleep is seen as virtuous, non-virtuous or neutral depending on the immediately preceding consciousness – the mind just prior to sleep.

        + - 2) Regret / contrition (skt.:kaukritya; tib.:‘gyod pa)

        • Regret can be either virtuous or non-virtuous depending on the object of regret. It is virtuous when one regrets over the negative things done in the past. In contrast, regretting a wholesome deed is non-virtuous.

        + - 3) General Analysis (skt.:vitarka; tib.:rtog pa)

        • It is the mind that explores an object – the things and events that make up our daily lives – but not in a deep analytical way. It is virtuous or non-virtuous depending on the motivation.

        + - 4) Subtle Analysis (skt.:vichāra; tib.:dpyod pa)

        • This mental factor explores the object in a detailed way. Like general examination the polarity of the mind of subtle analysis is determined by the object it is exploring. If the object is wholesome, the mind is wholesome and vice versa.
